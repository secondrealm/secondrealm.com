As I reflect on my journey back to college to complete my bachelor's degree, I am struck by the unexpected ways my studies have intertwined with my professional life and personal growth.

The decision to pursue labor studies was initially driven by its interdisciplinary approach—encompassing history, psychology, economics, law, anthropology, technology, and more. This diversity appealed to me deeply, as I’ve long been drawn to understanding the complexities of human experience from multiple angles.

What I didn’t anticipate, however, was how relevant this field would become in understanding current technological shifts—particularly in artificial intelligence and blockchain.

Viewing these modern developments through the lens of 20th-century technological transitions has given me a historical perspective that shapes how I interpret today’s digital world.

## The Jacquard Loom Connection

One of the most fascinating discoveries in my studies has been the Jacquard loom.

This textile machine represents an early intersection of technology and labor. Its use of punched cards laid the groundwork for computing and, eventually, digital art.

Seeing the connection between the Jacquard loom and my own work in Web3 as an artist was a turning point. It revealed a deeper lineage between industrial systems and contemporary creative technologies.

## Emotional Intelligence and Art

Alongside this, I’ve been studying emotional intelligence—particularly emotional vocabulary and literacy.

This exploration has influenced how I approach digital art. It’s pushed me to think beyond visual output and toward emotional depth—how work can express complex internal states while also reflecting broader social themes.

By integrating emotional intelligence into my practice, I aim to create work that bridges personal experience and shared human understanding.

## A Convergence of Disciplines

This combination—labor studies, computing history, emotional intelligence, and art—has led to a synthesis of ideas that continues to evolve.

I no longer see my role as simply creating visually engaging work. I see it as contributing to a broader conversation about society, technology, and human experience.

My academic work has deepened my creative practice, giving me a more informed and layered perspective on what I make and why.

## Closing Reflection

As I move forward after graduation, I’m interested in continuing to explore these intersections.

This journey—from studying labor and technology to integrating emotional intelligence into art—isn’t just academic. It’s personal.

It’s about understanding how we navigate a changing world—and finding ways to express that through the work we create.
