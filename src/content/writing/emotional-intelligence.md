Emotional Intelligence (EI) is gaining prominence as a crucial aspect of human psychology, challenging the traditional focus solely on cognitive ability.¹

Unlike the oversimplified interpretations often found in mainstream psychology, EI offers a framework for understanding how we perceive, express, and regulate emotions—shaping both personal growth and professional relationships.

## Emotional Literacy and Vocabulary

At the core of EI lies emotional literacy—the ability to identify and communicate emotions effectively.

A key component of this literacy is emotional vocabulary: the range of words we use to articulate how we feel. Expanding this vocabulary improves clarity in communication and deepens understanding, enriching how we relate to others.²

## Confronting My Own Gaps

In my initial exploration of emotional intelligence, I completed both introspective self-assessments and the Mayer-Salovey-Caruso Emotional Intelligence Test (MSCEIT).³⁴

Despite feeling confident—especially in empathy—the results revealed gaps in my ability to understand emotional blends.⁵

This area involves distinguishing between complex combinations of emotions—states that are often subtle and overlapping. Scoring below average in this domain highlighted a clear area for growth.

The results didn’t just confirm a weakness—they made it measurable.

Emotional blends, something I had already sensed as a challenge, became more concrete. This has also been reinforced through personal work with therapists and coaches. It’s clear that relying only on strengths like empathy and relationship management isn’t enough. Developing this deeper layer of understanding is essential.

## Practicing Emotional Intelligence

In my recent academic work, I’ve encountered several practices that support emotional literacy:

- Reading fiction⁶  
- Journaling⁷  
- Meditation⁸  

While I recognize the value of these practices, the real challenge lies in applying them intentionally—specifically focusing on emotional awareness within those routines.

It’s one thing to journal. It’s another to journal with precision around emotional nuance.

## The Challenge of Depth

I understand the importance of connecting with complex emotional states, but doing so consistently—especially internally—is difficult.

This is work that requires time, intention, and guidance. It’s not just intellectual—it’s experiential.

## Closing Reflection

My exploration of emotional intelligence is still early.

What this process has given me is awareness—especially around the complexity of emotional blends. That awareness opens up both difficulty and possibility.

Emotional intelligence isn’t something you arrive at. It’s something you practice.

And for me, this is the beginning of that practice.
