Shaolin Shoppe is a crypto artist, bass player, and one of the early voices in the trash art space.  

He entered crypto art before it gained mainstream attention, minting his first NFT for just $3. His work blends vaporwave, glitch, and experimental aesthetics.  

We talked about music, artistic influences, crypto, and how he approaches building a creative life.  

## What would 10-year-old Shaolin Shoppe think about you?

He’d probably be disappointed I’m not a cowboy.  

I grew up watching westerns and just wanted the hat, the horse, the whole thing. I didn’t really understand what being a cowboy meant—I just thought it looked cool.  

So yeah, he’d probably ask why I’m sitting at a computer all day.  

He might think playing bass is cool, but overall he’d probably just call me a nerd.  

## Which musicians shaped your taste?

Sublime was a big influence. Their basslines pulled me toward the instrument.  

I also got really into The Doors. I read everything I could about Jim Morrison when I was younger.  

## What kind of music NFTs are you working on now?

Earlier, most of what I made was improvised. I’d record loops, layer sounds, sometimes include dialogue from movies.  

Now I’m trying to be more intentional—actually writing and structuring songs.  

I’ve been jamming with my dad a lot. We built a makeshift studio at home. One of the first things I bought with NFT earnings was an amp for him.  

That moment mattered.  

## What’s your background in art?

Completely self-taught.  

I got into vaporwave early on and never really left it. It’s not just a music genre—the visual identity is just as important.  

Albums in vaporwave are judged as much by their aesthetic as by their sound.  

A lot of that influenced my approach to making art.  

## Where does the name Shaolin Shoppe come from?

The “Shoppe” part is a reference to *Floral Shoppe* by Macintosh Plus.  

The “Shaolin” part comes from kung-fu movies and Wu-Tang Clan.  

It just kind of came together.  

## Favorite kung-fu movie?

*Drunken Master* with Jackie Chan.  

He’s really young in it, and it’s a perfect mix of comedy and martial arts.  

## What themes are you exploring in your work?

I’ve been revisiting vaporwave aesthetics recently, especially through collaborations.  

I did a piece with Sgt. Slaughtermelon that centered around marble busts and philosophical figures.  

Beyond that, I’ve been exploring abstraction—color, pattern, motion.  

One piece I’m especially proud of is *Restless Seas*, inspired by the ocean.  

## You created something called an NFD—what is that?

It stands for “non-fungible dick.”  

It was meant as commentary.  

There was a trend where certain types of images were selling for a lot, and it created frustration among artists. I thought it was funny more than anything.  

So I flipped the idea—created a male version of a “thirst trap,” but in a pixelated, tongue-in-cheek way.  

It was both a joke and a critique.  

## How did you get into crypto art?

Honestly, I didn’t know much about crypto at all.  

I came in purely as an artist looking for a way to get paid. I spent about $3 to get started.  

The deeper understanding of crypto came later.  

In some ways, not knowing much at the beginning helped—I wasn’t obsessing over market fluctuations.  

## What’s your perspective on crypto as a system?

If people want crypto to become a real alternative, they have to stop constantly translating it into fiat.  

Adoption happens when you can use it directly—when it becomes intuitive.  

For me, crypto created freedom. I can work on my own time, focus on art, and not be tied to a traditional schedule.  

## What’s next for you?

Continuing to create.  

I want to explore more abstraction, but also expand into new directions—like blending kung-fu visuals with vaporwave aesthetics.  

I also want to support other artists more. I’m not trying to become a collector, but I do want to participate more actively in the community.  

## What advice would you give to newcomers?

Make good work.  

That sounds obvious, but it matters.  

Also, don’t ignore the sales side. Building relationships with collectors is part of the process.  

It might feel uncomfortable at first, but it’s not something to avoid—it’s part of sustaining your practice.  

Be respectful, don’t burn bridges, and contribute to the community.
