In general, my advice for keeping an associate's degree on a resume goes like this:

If your AA or AS is in the same exact major as your bachelor's (let's say psychology), then including it on your resume seems redundant to me. However, if your associate's is in a different major entirely (or even a related area with different names like early childhood education vs. psychology), it could be beneficial to showcase multiple areas of education.

It also adds another possible keyword for applicant tracking systems (ATS) and search optimization on platforms like LinkedIn.

My associate's falls into the “different major” category (computer science vs. social science). But I have a deeper and more meaningful reason for keeping it on my resume—and proudly hanging it on my wall.

## How My Associate's Degree Shaped My Life

Featuring my associate's degree on my resume is more than just demonstrating a diverse educational background. It represents a turning point—a transition from art school into a career in technology and design.

In 2000, when I returned to Hudson County Community College intending to take art classes, I found myself at a crossroads when the art program was discontinued.

That moment wasn’t just a setback—it was a pivot.

A counselor suggested web design as an alternative. With no prior coding experience, I made the decision to major in Computer Science. That choice set me on a completely new trajectory.

This degree gave me foundational skills in programming, web design, and development. The hands-on experience I gained became the base layer for everything that followed.

## The Role of Interdisciplinary Learning

My time at HCCC also included participation in an experimental Interdisciplinary Learning Community.

This approach resonated with me. In high school, I had been exposed to interdisciplinary thinking through the Palette Paradigm program—where art intersected with English, science, and math.

At HCCC, that expanded to include psychology, writing, and speech.

It deepened my understanding of how knowledge connects across domains.

This perspective carried forward into my career—helping me navigate roles at companies like MLB, HarperCollins, Google, and Twitter—and eventually leading me to explore the intersection of art and technology more intentionally.

## Why I Still Keep It

I’ve deliberately kept my Associate in Science in Computer Science on my resume—even after returning to Rutgers to pursue a Bachelor of Science in labor studies.

That degree represents more than coursework.

It represents adaptability.

It marks the moment I stepped into something unfamiliar—and found a path forward.

## Closing Reflection

The AS degree is a reminder that growth doesn’t always follow a straight line.

Sometimes the most important moves come from disruption—from being forced to change direction and choosing to move anyway.

For me, it stands as proof that stepping into the unknown can lead to something far more aligned than anything originally planned.
