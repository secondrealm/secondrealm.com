Dave Estes, also known as Reviiser, is an illustrator, musician, and crypto artist.  

From an early age, Dave had artistic ambitions. He attended art college but dropped out before graduating. After several years working as a lineman—one of the most dangerous jobs in America—while creating art on the side, he reached a breaking point.  

That’s when he discovered crypto art.  

Since 2018, Dave has created a wide range of work, but one of his most compelling series is *temporary paintings*—a daily practice where he paints a piece on the same canvas and immediately erases it.  

## What was Little Dave like?

Little Dave was a quiet kid who spent most of his time on the computer.  

I was always doodling. Even in school, I’d sit there drawing instead of doing assignments. That became a problem.  

Teachers would say, “you need to focus on your schoolwork,” but drawing was my escape.  

A lot of my early inspiration came from comics like *Tales from the Crypt*.  

## Were your parents supportive?

*Tales from the Crypt Issue 25*

They were, actually.  

During a parent-teacher conference, one teacher raised concerns about my drawings—zombies, horror imagery, that kind of thing.  

My parents weren’t worried. They understood it as expression. They were blue-collar, but very supportive.  

## What inspired your artistic direction?

Comics were a big influence, but surrealism changed everything.  

Dali was huge for me. I remember in ninth grade, one of my teachers asked if I had ever heard of Salvador Dalí.  

At that point, I hadn’t really been exposed to surrealism. Once I discovered it, I became fascinated with capturing things that don’t exist and making them feel real.  

## Did you see art as a viable career?

Honestly, no.  

My parents believed in me, but I didn’t believe in myself.  

I enrolled in art school but dropped out after half a semester. I just didn’t think I could make a living from it.  

## What was your experience working as a lineman?

I worked for Eversource through a contractor. I did horizontal directional drilling—basically drilling underground and trying not to hit gas lines.  

It’s a high-stress job.  

You work all day, come home exhausted, and go straight to bed. Then you wake up and do it again.  

There’s no space for anything else—not your mental health, not your family, not your art.  

Eventually, that pressure built up. I couldn’t keep going like that.  

## Was art your outlet during that time?

Yeah, it was my only outlet.  

I painted, drew, played guitar, and did local shows. But I was so drained from work that I couldn’t fully commit to anything.  

That’s when I started to feel like I was imploding.  

Around that same time, I discovered crypto art. It felt like an opportunity to finally pursue something meaningful.  

The community was a big part of that. I met people from all over the world, each with their own perspective.  

Coming from Connecticut, there wasn’t much of an art scene—unless you like paintings of boats and beaches.  

## You also build things—where did that come from?

I’ve always liked tinkering.  

My dad had a woodshop, and we built things together—cabinets, small objects, whatever we could think of.  

Later, I got into 3D printing and started experimenting more seriously. That’s how the cyberdeck came about.  

## What is the cyberdeck?

It’s a futuristic, completely impractical laptop.  

I kept asking myself, “how much can I put into this thing?”  

I wanted a mechanical keyboard, modular components, a cartridge-like system—it became this hybrid of sculpture and technology.  

At some point, I thought, “this is ridiculous,” but I had to finish it.  

And I did. It even got featured on Hackaday.  

## Let’s talk about the temporary paintings

The idea came from a conversation about burning journals.  

You write something, then destroy it as a way of releasing it.  

This is the same idea.  

I start with a blank canvas every day. No expectations. I create a complete piece, then erase it.  

The goal is to reach a point where I don’t want to erase it—and then do it anyway.  

## How do you erase them?

*Temporary Painting, 2021*

I keep a rag next to me and wipe it away immediately.  

If I look at the painting for too long, I might want to keep it. But that defeats the purpose.  

Destruction is part of the process.  

Interestingly, I spend more time looking at the canvas after it’s been wiped clean. That moment feels more meaningful.  

## Do you plan to mint these works?

I’ve photographed most of them, but I don’t feel compelled to mint them.  

That’s not the point.  

I’ve thought about minting the canvas itself—after everything has been erased—once I stop the series. But I’m not sure yet.  

## What are you working on now?

I’ve been focusing on ink and watercolor. That’s where I feel most comfortable.  

The temporary painting practice helped me loosen up. I’m less rigid now, more expressive.  

## What are your thoughts on trash art?

I love it.  

It’s not a formal collective—it’s a group of people connected by shared ideas.  

There are no rules. Anyone can be part of it.  

That’s what crypto art represents to me.  

In a way, everyone is a trash artist. Some people just don’t realize it yet.
