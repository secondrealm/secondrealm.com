The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act has brought significant changes to the tax landscape, especially for those dealing in digital assets. Under the amended Internal Revenue Code (IRC) 6050I [1], effective December 31, 2023, businesses and individuals engaging in transactions involving cryptocurrencies are now required to adhere to specific reporting requirements. This amendment targets transactions exceeding $10,000, whether in a single transaction or a series of related transactions.Subscribe

## Understanding the Law

In a pivotal move, the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021 ushered in a new era for asset management. This legislation expanded the existing $10,000 reporting threshold, historically a tool against money laundering in fiat transactions, to now include cryptocurrencies. This expansion not only mirrors the burgeoning significance of cryptocurrencies in our economy but also marks a concerted effort to infuse transparency into these digital transactions, much like their cash counterparts.

Primarily aimed at brokers and businesses, this shift holds particular resonance for artists. The definition of 'digital assets' now spans a wide spectrum, encapsulating various cryptocurrency forms and other digital values secured on cryptographic ledgers. Compliance becomes key when artists engage in transactions exceeding the $10,000 mark, whether through a single exchange or multiple interconnected dealings. Reporting these transactions to the IRS, via Form 8300, demands an intricate detailing of the payer's information, transaction amounts in USD, dates, and the nature of each transaction.

Moreover, the concept of 'related transactions' comes to the forefront. This notion binds multiple transactions from a singular source that cumulatively breach the $10,000 threshold, treating them as a unified transaction for reporting. Such a rule accentuates the necessity for meticulous record-keeping and astute reporting, ensuring adherence to the newly minted IRS regulations.

In essence, this legislative change is not just a legal formality but a transformative stride in acknowledging and regulating the ever-expanding digital currency landscape, a space where artists are increasingly staking their claim.Subscribe

## Is an Individual Selling NFTs Classified a “Business” by the IRS?

For artists selling their artwork as NFTs, understanding whether their activities constitute a 'business' is crucial for tax purposes. The IRS typically considers frequency, regularity, and profit motive when determining if an activity is a business.

### Key Considerations for Artists:

- **Frequency and Regularity:** Regular and consistent sales of artwork may be seen as business activity.  
- **Profit Motive:** If the primary goal is income generation, it's likely to be considered a business.  
- **Tax Implications:** Artists classified as a business must adhere to specific tax reporting requirements, which could include reporting large crypto-related transactions.  

## What This Means for NFT Transactions?

If you are an artist who frequently sells artwork and receives payments in crypto, especially if the transaction value exceeds $10,000, you might be subject to the same reporting requirements as other businesses. This includes filing necessary forms with the IRS and maintaining detailed records of transactions.

However, each artist's situation is unique, and many factors determine business classification. If you're unsure about your status, consulting with a tax professional is advisable to ensure compliance with IRS regulations and reporting requirements.

## 5 Tips for Artists

- **Keep Detailed Records:** Maintain accurate records of all art sales and transactions, noting dates, amounts, and payer details.  
- **Understand the Value:** Be aware of the cryptocurrency's value in USD at the time of each transaction to determine if it meets the reporting threshold.  
- **Understand the Ecosystem:** Stay informed about the regulatory environment, especially if you use third-party platforms or brokers for selling your art. Awareness of how these laws affect buyers and marketplaces can guide your decisions on where and how to sell your work.  
- **Consult Professionals:** Don’t hesitate to seek advice from tax experts or legal advisors, especially in ambiguous situations.  
- **Plan for Compliance:** While the direct impact on individual artists may be limited, being informed is crucial in the evolving cryptocurrency space. If you intend to expand your operations, understanding these regulations is key for future compliance and business planning.  

## Steps to Report

- **Gather Information:** Collect all necessary details from the payer at the time of the transaction.  
- **Fill Out Form 8300:** Complete the form with accurate transaction details. [2]  
- **File Timely:** Ensure you file the form within 15 days of the transaction.  
- **Notify the Payer:** Provide the payer with a written statement of the reported information by January 31 of the following year.  
- **Keep Copies:** Retain copies of all filings and payer statements for your records.  

The amendment to IRC 6050I is a significant development for artists and businesses dealing in digital assets. Understanding and complying with these new requirements is crucial to avoid penalties and ensure smooth operations. By staying informed and organized, artists can navigate these changes effectively and continue to thrive in the evolving digital art landscape.
