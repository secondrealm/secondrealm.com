Rob Prior is an extremely versatile artist—an illustrator, screenwriter, director, and storyboard artist whose career spans over three decades across comics, television, film, and gaming.  

In comics, he’s worked with major players like Marvel, DC, Image Comics, and Todd McFarlane. In film and television, his credits include *Star Wars: The Force Awakens*, *Terminator*, *Deep Space 9*, and *Game of Thrones*.  

He’s also well-known for his live painting performances—often painting with both hands, sometimes with his eyes closed, in front of massive crowds.  

## What would 8-year-old Rob think of you today?

I think he’d say I’m on the right path.  

At 8 years old, I was already being trained by my parents to become a painter. I never had another plan.  

Everyone in my family is an artist. My parents, grandparents—everyone. But none of them made it professionally. So they started training me early. By age 7, I was spending an hour a day in the studio.  

## When did you become a professional artist?

Very early.  

I started working locally at 12 or 13. I had already been training for years, so when opportunities came, I was ready.  

I remember my art teacher telling me, “we can’t teach you anything.” That moment gave me confidence—but it also made me work harder.  

## When did you start painting with both hands?

I was born right-handed.  

But at age 10, I had this realization—what if I lost my right hand? I wouldn’t be able to paint anymore. That wasn’t acceptable to me.  

So I stopped using my right hand entirely and trained my left. I did everything with it—writing, eating, playing sports—for six months.  

Then one day, I had both math homework and an art assignment due. I needed to move fast, so I picked up two brushes and started painting with both hands.  

## You also paint with your eyes closed?

Yeah, but that’s not something I practiced—it just happens.  

When I paint live, especially on stage with artists like Tech N9ne or Linkin Park, people think it’s choreographed. It’s not.  

Even with my eyes closed, I can see the painting in my mind. I know exactly where everything goes.  

There’s actually someone who wants to put me in a sensory deprivation tank and then have me paint afterward. I’m curious to see what would come out of that.  

## Do you consider yourself a performance artist?

People call me that, but the truth is—I paint the same way whether I’m on stage or alone in my studio.  

There’s no difference.  

I listen to loud music, move around constantly, and work on multiple pieces at once. My apprentices sometimes have to wear earplugs because of how intense it gets.  

The music changes depending on the piece—metal, rap, classical—whatever fits.  

## You have apprentices—what’s that like?

I have three apprentices, plus my son.  

Helping someone develop their own style is one of the hardest things to do.  

A lot of times, they’ll start creating work that looks like mine. That’s natural—we all emulate someone at first. I did the same thing with Larry Elmore.  

But part of my job is to tell them when they need to scrap something and start over. They need to find their own voice.  

## Do you draw inspiration from pop culture?

*Spider-Man Homecoming, Rob Prior*

Yes and no.  

I’m a huge fan of pop culture, but my favorite thing to paint is actually abstract work and human motion.  

When I do pop culture pieces, it’s often for fans. I do live streams every week where people suggest what I should paint. It usually spirals into chaos—and that’s part of the fun.  

That said, I don’t enjoy repeating the same subjects. I’ve painted Yoda more times than I ever wanted to.  

## How did you start performing live with musicians?

That started with Tech N9ne and Strange Music.  

In 2014, I created an art book for one of his albums—painting each piece live in the studio.  

Eventually, he asked me to join him on stage. I kept saying no because I had terrible stage fright.  

Finally, he convinced me. He told me I wouldn’t see the crowd because of the lights. That was a lie—I could see everything.  

I had a choice: face the crowd or go backstage and deal with my wife.  

I chose the crowd.  

## How did you transition into directing?

I got hired as an art director on a *Dungeons & Dragons* film early in my career.  

I quit my job and moved to Hollywood—but I had no idea what I was doing. I got fired pretty quickly.  

Instead of leaving, I stayed and learned everything I could about directing. I made short films, met the right people, and eventually directed *Painted Beauty*, starring Claire Holt and Clifton Collins Jr.  

## How did you get into NFTs?

I heard about NFTs around the same time as everyone else, but I didn’t fully understand them.  

A company called Mogul approached me to join their board. I figured—why not try something new?  

Since then, I’ve been creating NFTs by digitizing my physical paintings.  

For some pieces, I burn the original after minting. The idea is to create a single, definitive version—the NFT itself.  

## What are your thoughts on copyright and IP?

I see NFTs as originals.  

If you’re creating original art, you should be able to paint whatever you want. As long as it’s a one-of-one, it’s your creation.  

Where things get complicated is when you move into merchandise—prints, apparel, etc. That’s where IP issues come into play.  

## What’s next for you?

*Cobain, the 27 Club, Rob Prior*

I’m focusing on NFTs and exploring mobile-first projects.  

I also have a few surprise projects in the works. Once things normalize, I plan to return to gallery shows and start my next film.  

During COVID, I painted nonstop—150 pieces a year. I’m slowing that down now.
