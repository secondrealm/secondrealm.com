Since the age of 10, J.J Weinberg knew what he wanted to do. Exactly what he wanted to do. He told his mom that he was going to go to the American Academy of Art. Soon after, he won his first art competition, painting a “Just Say No” poster.

That’s how his journey began.

J.J. always had entrepreneurial aspirations. When he was 13, he bought 20 copies of “Spawn #1” dreaming that someday, he’d open a comic book shop.

He’s still working on it. But he enrolled in the Academy of Art thinking he’d become a comic book artist, studying under the iconic Alex Ross. There, the tutelage of Dr. Trapp helped J.J. develop his own unique style.

J.J. has accomplished a lot since college.

His painting “Midas’ Love - The Gold Kisser” won the Grand Prize award at the 2015 Pop-Up Art Festival, he was nominated for the Kennedy Artist Fellows Award in 2016, and his startup Artunity won the People’s Choice Award for the Startup of the Year in 2019.

## What would a 9-year-old J.J. think of himself today?

Man… A 9-year-old J.J. would say, “I told you so!”

Around the age of 10, I told my mom, “I’m going to be an artist.” I already knew that I was going to enroll in the American Academy of Art in Chicago. The great thing about it, she said, “yes, you are!” That was just fantastic. I loved her support.

And yeah, I called it. I felt like Sebastian in *The NeverEnding Story*. That’s what my life’s been like ever since I was a kid. Serendipitous event, after serendipitous event. 

## And you ended up at the American Academy of Arts, right?

Yup. Definitely. It was an interesting experience. The American Academy of Arts is a sort of commercial school. I have a degree in illustration. I wanted to draw comics. When I was around the age of 11 or 12, I had plans of opening a comic book store.

I grew up in an entrepreneurial family, so the school was a great fit for me. When I was 13, I bought 20 copies of Spawn #1. I still have them in mint condition.

The school had a couple of alumni. One of them was Alex Ross. You might be familiar with some of his work. He would come to the school, show us a few things, and get free models. He took photos of a few of my friends, and they ended up in one of his books.

## Did you learn more about art in school or out of school?

Probably out of school. But I did learn two really important things in art school.

Number one, I learned to trace. Most people think that’s the cardinal sin, but I have never drawn without a reference. Whether you’re doing something realistic or something hyper-stylized, get your lines on by tracing them first.

The other thing is a bit more philosophical.

My drawing professor, Dr. Trapp, had a saying that stayed with me to this day. First class, he comes in and says, “I’m not going to teach you how to draw. I’m going to ask you to tell me—how do you really see the world?”

And I think that’s such a beautiful thought.

## Can you tell me about the Cubs painting?

*Field of Dreams, J.J Weinberg*

You’re talking about the “Field of Dreams” painting I did for my dad. Yeah, that’s one of my works that went viral. But that’s not why I really love painting.

My dad and I never had a perfect relationship. We love each other, of course, but it’s had a lot of ups and downs. That painting was a kind of healing totem for me. The idea came naturally since my dad is a lifelong Cubs fan.

When I was growing up, my dad drank a lot. He had dreams of becoming a professional baseball player, but his parents didn’t let him pursue it. That created a lot of tension in his life.

He struggled with alcoholism and anger. I remember being afraid of baseball as a kid because of that. But I wanted to change our relationship.

So I made the painting and gave it to him. I recorded his reaction. You can really see something shift in that moment.

## That piece made it onto ESPN?

Yeah. I shared it in a few small Cubs Facebook groups. Then it started spreading. Bigger groups picked it up, then bigger pages, and eventually it made it to ESPN.

It was picked up by outlets like NBC and the Chicago Tribune. It even made it onto SportsCenter’s top plays. Gary Vaynerchuk saw it too.

## How did you meet Gary Vee?

I got in touch with his assistant through LinkedIn—Tyler, great guy. We started talking, and I sent him a painting I made for Gary.

A few days later, he asked if I could come into the office. I flew out to New York for a day just to meet him.

When I got there, Gary was bouncing between meetings. As he walked by me, he smacked me on the ass. So I joked, “That’s it, I’m filing charges.”

It caused a bit of a stir, but he just smirked and kept going. Classic Gary energy.

I didn’t want cameras or anything. No selfies. I just wanted the moment.

## How did the idea for Artunity come about?

*Liberty or Death, J.J Weinberg*

I was broke. I had a $150 bill due and no money. I needed to sell something.

I had a great illustration, but I didn’t know how to sell it quickly. So I thought—what if I crowdsource it?

I found 15 people, asked each for $10, and then randomly gave the artwork to one of them. It worked perfectly.

That’s when I realized—this could be a platform.

I got some angel investors and raised over $100,000. The hardest part was naming it. I went through 150 names before landing on Artunity.

The collectors get art. Artists get paid. Artunity.

## How does the platform work?

If you’re a struggling artist, Artunity gives you access to buyers without needing capital or marketing.

Artists set their own prices. Buyers contribute small amounts, and the platform randomly selects a winner to receive the artwork.

We take a small percentage. It’s a win for everyone.

## Can we talk about your wrestling project?

Yeah, my brother and I are building an NFT wrestling promotion called the Nonphysical Wrestling Federation.

We’ve created a whole cast of characters. One is the Million-Dollar Dogecoin Man. Another is Cheeto Santana.

Each character has a full backstory. We’re building a whole universe around it.
