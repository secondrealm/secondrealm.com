As a ten-year-old, Sgt. Slaughtermelon loved Wolverine and Boba Fett—and wanted to become a cartoonist.  

Today, he’s a pseudonymous psychedelic artist with a deep appreciation for abstract art, creative discipline, and self-directed learning.  

We talked about early influences, education, abstract art, and what it actually takes to enter the NFT space.  

## Who was 10-year-old Sgt. Slaughtermelon?

Ten-year-old me loved Wolverine and Boba Fett.  

I wanted to draw comic books. That was the dream—just making funny comics and being a cartoonist.  

I could draw Garfield and Sonic from memory, and at the time, I thought that was enough to make it.  

Looking back, I didn’t realize how much work it actually takes.  

If that version of me saw me today, I think he’d just say, “That’s it—I did it.”  

## How did art show up in your early life?

Art was always there, but one moment stands out.  

When I was in high school, a couple moved in next door. The guy worked at Barnes & Noble, and they gave us a stack of coffee table art books.  

These weren’t the kind of books kids usually get—full-page prints, art history, everything.  

That exposure changed things for me. It gave me access to work I probably wouldn’t have seen until much later.  

## Were your parents supportive?

In a balanced way.  

My dad believed computers were a more reliable path, but he didn’t try to shut down my creativity.  

He encouraged me to explore graphic design and digital tools. He even bought me Photoshop Elements early on.  

That ended up shaping my path—I pursued a degree in computer graphics, and in hindsight, it was the right move.  

## What draws you to abstract art?

It’s largely personal preference.  

I can appreciate classical work, but it doesn’t hold my attention the same way. Abstract art feels more engaging to me.  

What’s interesting is how recent abstract art actually is. It only really emerged about a century ago.  

The idea that the United States is older than abstract art still blows my mind.  

## How did your Art Blocks project come together?

It started with my Lazlo Lissitsky project.  

Someone suggested I check out Art Blocks, so I joined their Discord. At the time, there wasn’t a formal application—just experimentation.  

Later, when they introduced an application process, I applied.  

Jeff Davis reached out, and it turned out he had already collected some of my work. We started talking and eventually decided to collaborate.  

## Do you collect NFTs yourself?

Not really.  

On the rare occasions I’ve bought something, I’ve later questioned why I did it.  

So I’ve mostly stayed focused on creating rather than collecting.  

## What would you tell someone trying to get into NFTs?

Commit fully or don’t do it.  

If you’re hesitant to learn new platforms or don’t want to put in the effort to understand how everything works, it’s going to be difficult.  

No one is going to walk you through it step by step.  

You have to be willing to figure things out on your own.
