Frequently, we encounter clichés like this presented as sage advice. However, the truth is that making this a reality requires effort.

Let’s break this idea down by asking a set of questions artists should consider.

## Who Are “Them”?

Artists should define their audience more precisely than “everyone” or “people who buy my work.”

If possible, reach out to those who have already purchased your work. Did they buy it because you're friends? Because they connect with the work? Because of context?

Understanding this reveals how much of your traction is relationship-driven versus value-driven.

## What Does “Yearn” Mean?

What emotions does your work already evoke?

Are those the emotions you want to evoke?

Does that emotional response align with your intention as an artist—or is there a gap between what you make and how it’s received?

## What Does “See” Mean?

Where does your audience expect to encounter your work?

Physical galleries? Digital platforms? Social feeds?

Are you showing up in those places—and do those environments align with how you want your work to be experienced?

## What Does “Touch” Mean?

Where does interaction happen?

Is it through comments, newsletters, direct messages, in-person conversations?

Are you present in those moments of interaction—or are you absent where engagement actually happens?

## What Does “Own” Mean?

Why would someone want to own your work?

Is it for aesthetic value? Emotional connection? Investment? Status?

Do they want physical pieces, prints, or NFTs?

Understanding this changes how you present and distribute your work.

## The Reality Behind the Phrase

“Make them yearn to see, touch, and own your art” sounds simple.

But once you start unpacking it, it becomes complex and demanding.

This is why the phrase often feels easier to say than to execute.

## A Practical Starting Point

This process raises more questions than answers—and that’s the point.

Every artist is different. Every audience is different. There is no universal answer.

Your job is to define your answers.

Or, if you have the resources, to find people who can help you uncover them.

## Start Small

If this feels like a lot—it is.

Start small. Work through these questions over time.

Refine your understanding. Test your assumptions.

Eventually, you’ll begin to see what actually resonates.
