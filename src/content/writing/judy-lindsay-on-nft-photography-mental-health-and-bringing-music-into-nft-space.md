GIFs. Poems. Tweets. Burnt Banksy prints. Anything can be turned into an NFT. The NFT space has helped traditional artists transition into the digital world and, in some cases, make millions.  

However, one group of artists has lagged behind: photographers.  

Artists like Judy Lindsay are working to change that.  

From a commercial perspective, NFTs and photography are a natural fit. Digital photographers have long struggled to monetize their work due to oversaturation and challenges around intellectual property. NFTs offer a new path—enabling artists to sell individual pieces with verified ownership.  

A photographer like Judy can now make a living selling minted photographs.  

But for Judy, it’s not just about economics. Her journey is deeply tied to mental health, loss, and the role art plays in survival.  

## How did your NFT journey get started?

Everything started on February 13, when my friend Crawley reached out to me out of the blue.  

He said he had been studying NFTs for a year during the pandemic and wanted to mint one of my photographs.  

To be honest, I had no idea what he was talking about. But I trusted him and sent over my favorite image—the best piece I had created during lockdown.  

Crawley is part of the Enigma Bazaar Collective in Colorado. They have both physical and virtual galleries, so he knew what he was doing.  

He minted the piece, posted it, and three days later, it sold.  

That’s what got everything started. I give him full credit.  

## What about the NFT photography community?

*The Peak, Judy Lindsay*

Art got me through the pandemic—or the apocalypse, as I call it.  

I’ve been a photographer my whole life. I have a darkroom, my gear—everything. But during lockdown, I couldn’t engage with photography the way I normally would.  

When I entered NFTs, I became fascinated by the energy—watching bidding wars unfold in real time.  

Daniel Stagner was one of the first people I connected with. I was added to several group chats, and that sense of community gave me the strength to keep going.  

The money was great, but it wasn’t the point. It was about creative energy.  

At one point, we realized that many photographers didn’t even know NFTs existed. So we created a Discord community.  

Within two months, it grew to over 1,200 members.  

It’s a supportive space—full of love, kindness, and zero tolerance for scams.  

## Where does that sense of love and kindness come from?

Honestly, it comes from pain.  

Depression. Anxiety. Hospitalization.  

The last decade has been incredibly difficult. I lost my mother unexpectedly four years ago. That changed everything overnight.  

Last year, I also had to put down my horse—she was only 13. That experience broke me.  

My anxiety resurfaced in a major way.  

The only thing that kept me grounded was art—and the NFT community.  

Before NFTs, photography saved me. Now, this space continues to do that.  

## What’s more profitable—NFTs or prints?

NFTs. No question.  

I recently sold a photo for $500. That was a personal record.  

During the pandemic, I sold two physical prints for $30 each. To do that, I had to go to a print shop, then the post office, exposing myself to risk the entire time.  

All that effort for $60? It just wasn’t sustainable.  

NFTs remove that friction entirely.  

## What do you think about people who criticize NFTs?

I don’t have much patience for it.  

If someone starts criticizing NFTs, I’ll listen for a second—but most people just don’t understand the space.  

Everyone is benefiting from blockchain technology—miners, businesses, artists.  

I was interviewed by *The Guardian*, and I described myself as the Pied Piper of the NFT community.  

I’ve been an artist my whole life, but only now can I actually sustain myself financially.  

Even my brothers—who are successful artists—struggled during the pandemic. I introduced them to NFTs, and suddenly they had a new path.  

## What were you like at 12 years old?

*Wonderland, Judy Lindsay*

I was always creating. Drawing, writing, experimenting.  

At 11, I wrote an entire book—beginning, middle, and end. It was about a bunny. I recently rediscovered it and might turn it into an NFT.  

At 13, I was deeply depressed.  

But art pulled me out of that. It gave me something to hold onto.  

## You mentioned mental health—can you expand on that?

My father was a psychiatrist, and when I was 13, he put me on antidepressants.  

In hindsight, I don’t think I needed them. I think I was over-medicated for most of my life.  

More recently, I started taking Celexa for anxiety, and that’s been helpful.  

Looking back, anxiety—not depression—was the core issue.  

## How does that show up in your work?

My work is about joy.  

I don’t want to put negative energy into the world. I want to create things that uplift people.  

That feels like my purpose—spreading love and kindness through art.  

## What about your work outside of art?

I’ve been very active in social causes. During the BLM protests, I helped connect detained photographers with legal support.  

That visibility came with risks. I received death threats and had to remove personal information from the internet.  

Being a public figure adds complexity.  

## What’s your radio show about?

I host a music show focused on roots rock and soul. It airs on KBC in Seattle.  

We have over 70,000 listeners, and it’s interactive—I take requests live.  

The show has connected me with artists like Eric Bibb and Clinton Fearon, as well as emerging musicians.  

It’s another way of supporting creativity.  

## Are you bringing music into your NFT work?

Yes.  

I’m collaborating with DJ Burn One on a project that combines music and visual art. The piece will include a track alongside a framed original artwork.  

It will be released on Foundation.  

Most of my success in NFTs has come from collaborations, so you can expect more projects like this.
