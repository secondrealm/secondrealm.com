When he was ten years old, Randy Ortiz—also known as WG—was obsessed with Bruce Lee and kung-fu culture.  

That early fascination shaped not only his interests, but also how he shows up for others today.  

Now, he’s a crypto artist and part of the NFT collective *When Giants Meet*, balancing creative work with a full-time career and family life.  

## Who was ten-year-old WG?

At ten, I was fully into Bruce Lee.  

I was running around the house punching and kicking everything. I even had a Bruce Lee skateboard that I’d sit on while watching his movies.  

I was probably more active than most kids. That interest led me into taekwondo as well.  

## Did Bruce Lee influence who you are today?

Definitely.  

In those films, he was always standing up for himself or others, and that stuck with me.  

Even now, I try to help people—especially newcomers—because I remember what it feels like to be lost at the beginning.  

## Are you full-time in crypto art?

Not exactly.  

I have a full-time job, but I still consider myself a full-time artist.  

I’m balancing three different tracks: working in print, creating NFT art, and making my own personal work. On top of that, I’m a husband and a father of three.  

## How do you manage your time?

I don’t rely on strict schedules.  

Time is valuable, so I just fit art in wherever I can. When the kids are asleep, I work. Early mornings, I work. Between tasks, I work.  

It’s about finding balance rather than forcing structure.  

## When did you first get into art?

Fourth grade.  

I had a science project on chameleons and couldn’t find a reference image, so I decided to draw one from memory.  

It was different from anything I had done before. When I finished, my mom encouraged me and said it was really good.  

That moment stuck with me. That’s when I realized I actually loved creating.  

## Did you pursue art formally?

Yes, gradually.  

I went from a regular elementary school into a junior high that emphasized art more. That’s when I started building my own characters and illustrations.  

By seventh grade, I knew this was what I wanted to do long-term.  

In high school, I joined an “art house” program where I explored multiple mediums—charcoal, watercolor, oil painting, and more.  

I also went through phases: graffiti in ninth grade, photography in tenth.  

Art was always there, just evolving.  

## What did you study in college?

Graphic design and advertising.  

I initially considered photography, but I didn’t enjoy working with models. Design felt more natural—creating something from nothing.  

## How did you get into crypto?

I wasn’t deeply involved at first.  

I had heard about Bitcoin around 2016 or 2017, but didn’t pay much attention.  

Later, I got into TRON and started doing design work within that ecosystem. That was my entry point into creating in crypto.  

From there, I discovered NFTs and realized I could build and share my own work in this space.  

## What was your first real breakthrough moment?

Finding a platform where I could post my work and actually get paid for it.  

Creary felt like a “crypto Tumblr” to me—it gave me a place to experiment, share, and realize that this could be something real.
