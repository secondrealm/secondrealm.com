## Book Note

### Reaction: 24-hours after reading

Books like this are what I call anchor books. Their existence on my book shelf represents an idea I was drawn to. 

I've long admired Cal Newport's work. Though I admittedly found it hard to adopt a deep work methodology in my previous professional endeavors because so much of it was driven by shallow work. 

However, I did employ deep work processes in my personal life over the years. But I never systematized it for anything but personal growth and learning before.

I'm about to begin a scholarly journey. One that will have outsized impact on my personal and professional life over the next 4-5 years. 

So, I reread this to put me back in the right frame of mind. 

My initial goal is simple: use the next 90-days to practice deep work methods that work for me before entering an institution where a thesis and dissertation are the final outputs. As Newport puts it on page 71, "systematically develop your personal ability to go deep-and by doing so, reap great rewards."

Given that Cal is a highly-regarded academic who used these methods in his own pursuits effectively, it seems like a good place to start.

## Marginalia

### Notes I scribbled into the margins and Quotes I highlighted that still seem important to me 24-hours later 

- page 7, does automation via AI give us more time to do deep work? where before the need was to be connected in order to read email, can that now be automated? or is it still a matter of simply setting aside time to email and being vigilant about the boundary one sets?

- page 8, "Our work culture's shift toward the shallow..." does automation allow for more deep work and do the humanities teach us the skills to do that deep work well?

- page 13, "to remain valuable in our economy... you must master the art of quickly learning complicated things." and "to succeed you have to produce the absolute best stuff you're capable of producing-a task that requires depth"

- page 16, "minimize the shallow in my life" and "three to four hours a day, five days a week, of uninterrupted and carefully directed concentration"

- page 17, love listening to the yankees on the radio (reaction to Cal listening to the Nationals game on the radio.)

- page 18, can Second Realm be a digital version of my personal Bollingen Towers? the place Jung used as a retreat for his work away from his practice. 

- page 26, "there's a premium to being the best. if you're in a marketplace where the consumer has access to all performers, the consumer will choose the very best." This is essentially the "the superstar effect" and I'm curious if this phenomenon also explains that at the highest levels of sports and academia why the tiniest advantage has outsized impacts. And does this potentially add value to the idea that a background in the humanities becomes that value add when intelligence and capability differences are marginal. See: Nobel Prize winning scientists with arts/humanities practice. 

- page 27, "those who can work creatively with intelligent machines and those who are starts in their field" made me think of Range by David Epstein. Specifically where he talks about Centaurs vs Experts vs AI in the chess challenge. 

- page 31, "the absurdity of the now common idea that exposure to simplistic, consumer-facing products...somehow prepares people to succeed in a high tech economy." makes me reconsider my thoughts on AI in the classroom and also whether AI literacy is really the answer. 

- page 33, "Deep Work Helps You Quickly Learn Hard Things" essentially, slow is smooth. and smooth is quick. 

- page 43, go to the library w/ printed studies, read them, take notes, have AI transcribe the notes, then ask questions about your notes.

- page 45, "the type of work that optimizes your performance is deep work" and "the deep workers among them will outproduce you" so deep work seems to be a good predictor of future performance. In Scott Adams' UI for Reality, this would be a good system to use to help predict a better subjective reality. 

- page 60, Does automation upend our tendency to choose behaviors in the work environment that are easiest in the moment? Re: Newport's "Principle of Least Resistance" and does that then give us more time to deep work at work? will employers allow for employees to do deep work? 

- page 64, faux busyness, look productive even if you aren't. I remember reading somewhere that it's more acceptable in a work environment that someone be nose deep in their phone (potentially scrolling socials) rather than reading a book. Seems to be a similar pattern.

- page 69, "deep work is at a severe disadvantage in a technopoly" b/c it requires a rejection of what is "new and high-tech" not sure how to balance this idea being pro-ai/tech while also craving deep work

- page 70, what's bad for business is good for you b/c "the myopia of your peers and employers uncovers a great personal advantage." as "depth will become increasingly rare and therefore increasingly valuable" which is why I keep coming back to the idea that as this becomes more valuable, we'll see a rise in employers wanting to hire technically capable people with backgrounds in the humanities b/c the humanities provide depth in skills that will become more valuable over time. Not a fully-formed idea, but still working on it. 

- page 77, "our brains instead construct our worldview based on *what we pay attention to*" this is exactly the point of Adams' call to accept the frame of subjective reality. The individual controls the inputs and therefore their worldview. It's malleable. "who you are, what you think, feel, and do, what you love-is the sum of what you focus on" (Rapt, Gallagher)

- page 85, taking it a step further, intent matters. be thoughtful about the content you choose to focus on b/c "the content of what we focus on matters". Question: does this indicate that certain content can do more to amplify impact of deep work? Probably matters more on an individual level than can be measured in a standardized way.

- page 86, "build your life around flow produced by deep work is a proven path to deep satisfaction" in the aggregate. Isn't this delayed gratification in action? The marshmallow test. By avoiding the busyness look of shallow work performance in the short term in favor of a future more satisfied state. 

- page 87, deep work is a return to "order and sacredness essential to creating meaning." Curious if engaging in my Catholic roots in a more meaningful way is also a return to the metaphysical. Thinking about how the Dalai Lama suggests not finding a new religion but exploring the one of your culture. 

- page 87, "the craftsman" I've been thinking a lot about how AI allows us to return to craft by automating the human calculator aspects of our work life. 

- page 90, "your work is craft" a return to meaning and finding meaning in your everyday work. a separate take from my return to craft. 

- page 91, "you don't need a rarified job; you need instead a rarified approach to your work" and "it follows that to embrace deep work in your own career, and to direct it toward cultivating your skill, is an effort that can transform a knowledge work job from a distracted, draining obligation into something satisfying-a portal to a world full of shining, wondrous things." Something I hadn't considered as I mull over what to do next in my career. Perhaps I've been going about a job search all wrong. 

- page 100, "you have a finite amount of willpower that becomes depleted as you use it" such that you need to move past pure willpower and into routines, rituals and habits.

- page 102, "you need your own philosophy for integrating deep work into your professional life" relates to the depth philosophy (i.e monastic, bimodal, rhythmic). I'll likely use a hybrid approach correct for the context. I already have a robust set of tools to use in this case as practiced in my personal growth and learning life (morning routine, schedule blocking, walking and thinking, journaling, etc). I just need to be able to transfer certain habits to be used for professional means of deep work. 

- page 108, during this part of the book there's discussion about the role of social media and I remember thinking about how over the last 6 years, my community on Twitter/X has been a source for stimulating thinking. I just need to get better about making that the central use case for it when it comes to deep work. (It's entertainment too, but I also need to be more clear about my boundaries and intentions there as well.)

- page 111, "the chain method" = showing up every day but also means the focus becomes keeping the chain going and not necessarily the weight of the work itself. It's freeing to show up and do the work regardless. Not about being perfect each day, but working at it each day. 

- page 117-119, Ritualize, "think like artists but work like accountants" reminded of the story of Eminem showing up 9-5, grabbing lunch, going home after work. 

- page 121, "Make Grand Gestures" that seems like fun but also like "grand" can be interpreted many ways including small but impactful gestures that are one-offs. 

- page 136, "Discipline #1: Focus on the Wildly Important" reminds me of "The One Thing". What's the one thing that makes everything else easier or unnecessary? Do that. 

- page 137, Discipline #2: Lead vs Lag measures. I need to get better at identifying lead measures. Lead measures turn your attention to improving the behaviors you directly control. Where Lag measures what already happened. Good information. But that's it.

- page 139, Discipline #3: Keep a compelling scoreboard. Gamification. 

- page 140, Discipline #4: Create a Cadence of Accountability. Sounds like a good idea. Need to dive into the 4DX framework some more. 

- page 142, be lazy and use downtime to your favor. I've been good at this over the years. Developed a practice of just thinking. Remembered reading something that said, when was the last time you just sat and think. And ever since then, I try to take time to just do that. Not directed thinking. Just letting the brain meander without judgment. The opposite of mindful meditation. 

- page 152, create a shut down ritual for work. I've never been particularly good at this. Could be something to build on going forward. 

- page 159, take breaks from focus instead of taking breaks from distraction. it's a frame shift. the idea is to give into distraction but on your terms. 

- page 167, I have a unique ability to quickly drop into a state of concentration. Never thought of it as a superpower, but it has been a useful tool when I need to get shit done. Perhaps, I can use it more thoughtfully instead of in intense moments. Scheduling it so to speak. 

- page 168, "keep self-imposed deadlines right at the edge of feasibility" I like this for obvious reasons. It stretches without being too overwhelming. 

- page 170, "productive meditation" another tool I've been using for years but didn't know had a name. 

- page 177, I have aphantasia so visualizing is not a great tool for me. i have to use my other senses as memory. Wonder if the best memory experts instead have a hyper-intense mind's eye. Or as Sherlock Holmes would refer to it, the mind palace. 

- page 188, love the story about the farmer Pritchard and the depth with which he considered whether to buy hay bales or have a machine that does it. He chose to buy so that he can focus on making sure his soil health was center stage in his decision making process. And bailing hay would take him away from that. 

- page 191, the craftsman approach to choosing tools vs the any-benefit approach. Give me pause to consider how I choose which tools to implement in my own life. 

- page 194, Cal Newport "pursues two important goals, one centered on being an effective teacher in the classroom and an effective mentor to [his] graduate students, and another centered on being an effective teacher" I found it interesting that his writing is not a goal. Has me wondering where his book writing fits into all of this? Also, what are my goals re: Deep Work? Probably need to suss that out. 

- page 202, low-impact activities are the shallows

- page 209, "they're just products, developed by private companies, funded lavishly, marketed carefully, and designed ultimately to capture then sell your personal information and attention to advertisers." made me think about whether AI tools are even more insidious than that. 

- page 210, guilt free hours before and after the day. find more leisure time especially as the "low-brow attention economy" has reduced the quality of it. The way I see it is: I've already reduced my social media use to two platforms only. X and TikTok. Though I could get more mindful about when I use them. One is purely entertainment based. The other is news and connection. 

- page 213, structured hobbies are good for evenings and weekends. "they generate specific actions with specific goals to fill your time." this is true and I'd like to find a way to do this more. I already have an evening reading routine. So that's set. But really I need a good weekend hobby. I used to play softball, but I want something like that but is more fitnessy. Maybe hiking now that it's getting warmer. 

- page 218, drain the shallows. I guess I did this quite well in my professional life. I blocked time in my calendar with DNS (do not schedule). I hard-capped meetings at 15 minutes. I required agendas for meetings longer than 15 minutes. I used the hallways to get business done instead. 

- page 221, "treat shallow work with suspicion"

- page 222-233, schedule every minute of your day, but be flexible. This doesn't really work for me. I think I need to schedule weekday mornings and evenings and then let the middle be more flexible. Need to think more about why I'm resistant to this idea. 

- page 235, I'm looking for a company and place that knows the importance of deep work. 

- page 236, "Fixed schedule productivity" I can be very good about shutting down my work at 5:30 PM. Especially as I very often don't have my phone with me by my side. This I can practice right now. 

- page 241, "meta-habit" simple to adopt broad in its impact. Want to find more of these. 

- page 245 "sender filter" for emails, or don't even include an email on the website. 

- page 263, "the deep life, of course, is not for everybody. It requires hard work and drastic changes to your habits." and "struggle to deploy your mind to its fullest capacity to create things that matter." I actually think many of my habits are in line with this. I just need to adopt it to my professional life.
