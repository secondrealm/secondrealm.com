When he was seven years old, Robness loved watching MTV, listening to Nirvana and Billy Idol, and dreamed of becoming a rock star.  

Today, he’s a visual artist—and widely considered the godfather of Trash Art.  

In a way, he did become a rock star.  

We talked about music, vaporwave, spirituality, introversion, and how experimentation shapes his work.  

## Who was seven-year-old Robness?

Seven-year-old me was obsessed with MTV.  

I was trying to figure out who Billy Idol was, who Michael Jackson was, why MC Hammer felt like a demigod.  

Music was everything to me.  

When Nirvana broke out, it felt like the world flipped upside down. That shaped me in a big way.  

If that version of me met who I am today, we’d feel like different people—but we’d still connect through music.  

## Did you play instruments growing up?

Yeah, guitar.  

I got my first guitar at thirteen, and that changed everything. That’s when I really went all-in on music.  

## Do you still play?

Absolutely.  

If my house caught fire, I’d grab two things: my laptop and my guitar—the same one I’ve had since I was thirteen.  

## You seem extroverted—were you playing in bands?

Funny enough, I’m extremely introverted.  

Like, walking into a room full of people can be terrifying for me.  

I try to carry myself well, but I’m not as comfortable socially as people think.  

That said, I did play in bands. In high school, my friends and I were all into music and filmmaking. We’d hang out behind the library, sharing ideas.  

I played in a few punk bands—and even a Christian rock band at one point.  

## What role did high school play in shaping you?

It was formative.  

I had a friend who was obsessed with film and showed me everything I needed to see. That helped me understand culture early on.  

That mix of music, film, and hanging out with creative people really shaped how I think.  

## Let’s talk about vaporwave—what drew you to it?

Before NFTs, I found vaporwave.  

The music felt like distorted ‘80s nostalgia, and I loved it immediately. But the visuals—that’s what really got me.  

It was this fusion of sound and aesthetic that felt completely new.  

What fascinated me most was how strict the culture was. If your visuals didn’t match the aesthetic, the music didn’t count as vaporwave.  

There was this high standard for cohesion.  

And the community was strong too—forums, subreddits, people actively engaging with the art.  

## You’ve explored music, NFTs, writing… what drives that experimentation?

I just like trying things.  

When something interests me, I want to see if I can do it.  

It started with music, then I got into DJing. After that, I got curious about writing and ended up publishing a book.  

It’s just curiosity.  

## What’s next for you?

I want to start working on bigger, more collaborative projects.  

I’ve been doing this mostly on my own for a long time, but now I’m interested in building with other artists and seeing what happens.
