## Found Art and Junk Art — Bucking Tradition

This article traces the origins of Trash Art through earlier movements like Found Art and Junk Art. These foundations set the stage for understanding Trash Art in its contemporary, digital form.  

This is Part I of a two-part series.  


## The Beginning: Found Art

In 1917, Marcel Duchamp submitted *Fountain* to an exhibition held by the Society of Independent Artists.  

The work—a urinal signed “R. Mutt”—is widely considered the first piece of Found Art.  

It was rejected. Hidden from view.  

That reaction says everything about the moment:  

The object wasn’t the issue. The idea was.  


## Early Foundations

Even before Found Art became formalized as a movement in the 1950s, artists were already experimenting with its core ideas.  

Kurt Schwitters, a German Dadaist, created collage works using discarded materials—newspapers, wood scraps, debris.  

His philosophy, which he called *merz*, blurred the line between art, process, and life itself.  

His most ambitious work, *Merzbau*, transformed his home into a living, evolving installation built from found objects. It was later destroyed in World War II.  

The idea persisted:  

Destruction could be reassembled into meaning.  


## From Found Art to Junk Art

As artists pushed further, the use of discarded materials evolved.  

Robert Rauschenberg combined found objects with abstract painting, producing works like *Bed* (1955).  

This marked a shift—from simply presenting found objects to transforming them into new compositions.  

Jean Tinguely extended this idea with kinetic sculptures like *Homage to New York* (1960), which famously self-destructed.  

The artwork didn’t just use discarded materials—it returned to them.  

César Baldaccini pushed the material transformation further by compressing junked cars into sculptural forms.  

Across these works, a pattern emerges:  

- find  
- transform  
- recontextualize  


## Trash Art as Continuum

Trash Art doesn’t emerge in isolation.  

It builds on a lineage:

- Duchamp’s readymades  
- Dadaist collage  
- Junk Art’s material transformation  

Each step expands what counts as art—and who gets to define it.  

## Provocation and Material

Trash Art has always been controversial.  

Damien Hirst’s *A Thousand Years* (1990) exemplifies this.  

Using a cow’s head, maggots, flies, and a bug zapper, the work stages a cycle of life and death in real time.  

It’s unsettling.  

It forces confrontation.  

That’s part of the function.  

Discarded or abject materials become the vehicle for meaning.  


## The Digital Turn

In digital media, Trash Art takes on new form.  

Instead of physical debris, artists work with:

- existing images  
- corrupted files  
- remixed media  

Here, Trash Art intersects with Glitch Art—where errors, distortions, and data corruption become aesthetic tools.  

Michael Betancourt’s *The Kodak Moment* (2013) demonstrates this approach, fragmenting archival footage into a layered, unstable visual experience.  

The logic remains the same:  

Take what exists. Break it. Rebuild it.  


## Toward Crypto Art

As more artists move into digital environments, these traditions evolve again.  

Found Art becomes remix culture.  
Junk Art becomes recombination.  
Glitch Art becomes a native language.  

This convergence sets the stage for Trash Art in the NFT era—where ownership, authorship, and originality are re-examined in real time.

## Closing Thought

Trash Art is not a break from tradition.  

It’s a continuation of a question that keeps resurfacing:  

What happens when the material doesn’t matter anymore—but the framing does?
