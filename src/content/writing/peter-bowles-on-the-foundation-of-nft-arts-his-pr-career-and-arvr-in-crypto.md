Peter Bowles is a seasoned PR specialist, an early adopter of online marketing, a pioneer of viral marketing, and the founder of NFT Arts.  

From setting up an account for the youngest blogger in the world to creating a Bitcoin college foundation for a baby, Peter Bowles is known for his PR stunts. He was one of the early adopters of online marketing and managed to create several viral campaigns beginning in the early 2000s.  

Peter likes to be early on things, so it’s no wonder that he already dipped his toes into the world of crypto art.  

While most people would just open a crypto exchange, Peter’s plans are much more ambitious. He wants to open a gallery—an NFT gallery. One that would show people what NFTs are and give everyday individuals a way to explore crypto art.  

## Can you explain what NFT Arts is?

*Salmon LEE, kokoko*

Yeah, no problem—especially since a lot of people think we just showed up on Twitter asking people to share their art.  

NFT Arts is a virtual NFT gallery I’ve been working on since October. Artists can post their work and attract potential buyers. Ever since I discovered NFTs, I’ve been looking for a way to get involved in the space. I’m still relatively new to it.  

Initially, I thought about opening an exchange, but I wanted to do more than that. That’s why I chose the gallery route.  

My main goal is to open the eyes of everyday people and help them understand NFT art. The NFT Arts website is designed to give people a starting point into NFTs and crypto art as a whole.  

## Are you building your VR world?

For now, it’s a standard web experience, with background information on each piece in the gallery. But yes, we’re planning to build a full virtual environment on cyber.io.  

I’ve worked in virtual reality for years. Eventually, I’d like to create a fully immersive VR experience.  

I also want to build VR exhibitions for institutions. Imagine if the Guggenheim wanted to host an NFT exhibition—they could call us and we’d build it.  

## What did you do before NFT Arts?

I’ve always been drawn to media. I actually studied business and economics in college, but once I became interested in sociology, I dropped business.  

Sociology gives you a way to understand patterns in the world and anticipate where things are going. That helped me a lot in my career.  

While I was still in college, I did one of my first PR stunts. My cousin had a baby, and instead of sending a card, I created a blog for her.  

Blogging was still new at the time, and I labeled it “the youngest blogger in the world.” Overnight, it took off.  

One of the people who commented was Evan Williams, the creator of Blogger. The story spread quickly—even before we had a word for “viral.”  

## You were treating marketing as conceptual art?

Exactly.  

After college, I became a publicist. I ran a PR agency called Dynamite PR with a partner. We worked on campaigns across the world, including more than 100 Kickstarter projects.  

After about a decade, we sold the agency. Around that time, I also minted the article announcing my exit from the marketing world.  

## Are you comfortable talking about your recent time off?

Yeah, I’m happy to talk about it.  

For the first time in my life, I hit a wall. Right when I started working in NFTs, I had a breakdown and had to take six weeks off.  

It made me realize how important it is to take mental health seriously. You don’t always see burnout coming.  

During that time, I even reached out to Steve Wozniak. I had met him before and still had his card. I sent him an email explaining what I was going through.  

He replied. He was busy, of course, but he sent me a link to a song that really helped me reset.  

I’m in a much better place now. The support from family, friends, and the NFT community has made a big difference.  

## What’s your ultimate business goal?

I’d love to open a real-world NFT gallery in London. A place where people can walk in and experience crypto art firsthand.  

Some pieces would be physical displays, others would be 3D, and some would be immersive VR experiences.  

Long-term, I think augmented reality will be the main way people interact with digital art.  

Eventually, companies like Apple will release advanced AR glasses, and that will change everything.  

## How did you get in touch with the RZA of the Wu-Tang Clan?

Do you know the Cameo app? I used it to find someone who could help promote NFT Arts.  

I thought RZA would be a great fit since Wu-Tang has always been forward-thinking. I even consider their $2 million album a kind of proto-NFT.  

So I reached out, asked him to give a shout-out to NFT artists, and he was into it.  

## What kind of art do you have in the gallery?

Some pieces are commissioned, others are donated.  

For example, a photographer I know—who has worked with people like Steve Jobs, Tim Cook, and Elon Musk—donated some of his work.  

Photography is still underrepresented in the NFT space, so that’s something we’re excited about supporting.  

## What is The Satoshi Collection?

It’s a traveling gallery owned by NFT Arts.  

The idea is to build a collection of important NFT works and exhibit them in both physical and virtual spaces.  

Part of the collection is for sale, and part is held long-term. But every piece will be displayed in real-world and VR exhibitions over time.  

## What does your gallery bring to the world of NFT?

I want to help bridge NFTs into the real world.  

We’re planning VR exhibitions and eventually physical exhibitions—possibly in places like Paris.  

We’re also aligned with GLAM institutions—Galleries, Libraries, Archives, and Museums.  

The goal is to make NFT art accessible, understandable, and integrated into cultural spaces.
