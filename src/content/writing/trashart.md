## Note from a "Trash Art" Pioneer

*Last edited: Feb 25, 2024*

It might not surprise some, but four years ago I faced criticism, bans, and censorship for creating homages, remixes, and what was derogatorily labeled “trash” art.  

From February 2020 to June 2021, artists like myself, Robness, Max Osiris, and others were called frauds, thieves, scammers—accused of devaluing the space.  

For me, it culminated in a permanent ban from SuperRare in September 2020, simply for threatening to remix a Hackatao. That moment captured what many early “trash” artists were experiencing.  

Despite that, I kept building.  

From that tension, Trash Art emerged.

---

## The Emergence of "Trash Art"

Trash Art didn’t begin as rebellion for its own sake.  

It started as a response.  

At the time, gatekeepers in the space believed that derivatives, remixes, and so-called “low-effort” works devalued art. Artists engaging in those practices were pushed out.  

In response to Robness being banned for a remix of the 64-gallon toter, I created *Art Was Here*.  

It wasn’t an obvious remix—but if you knew, you knew.  

It passed platform rules while still making the point.  

That was the nature of the movement early on: conversation through form.  

---

## Trash Art: One Year Later

By early 2021, the narrative had shifted.  

I hosted conversations with both supporters and critics of the movement—documenting its growth and evolving meaning.  

What started as conflict was turning into discourse.  

---

## The Evolution of Trash Art

Over time, Trash Art developed into two distinct but related expressions:

- the movement  
- the meme  

Each evolved differently, but both shaped the culture.

### The Movement

At its core, the movement focused on:

- challenging gatekeeping  
- resisting censorship  
- embracing remix culture  
- expanding access for artists  

It wasn’t a centralized group.  

It was a shared stance.  

Artists used their work to question who gets to define value, ownership, and legitimacy in decentralized systems.  

Over time, these ideas spread beyond “trash art” itself and became embedded in broader Web3 culture.  

Remixing, derivatives, and open experimentation—once criticized—became normalized.  

A clear example:  

In February 2021, building on remix principles, I created *Unofficial Punks* and helped spark the Alt-Punk movement.  

By mid-2021, the broader NFT space had fully embraced derivatives.  

What was once rejected became standard practice.  

---

### The Meme (The Toter)

Alongside the movement, a symbol emerged:  

The 64-gallon toter.  

Originally created by Robness, it became an icon—remixed, parodied, reinterpreted.  

It evolved into a visual shorthand for everything the movement represented:  

- anti-mainstream energy  
- rejection of traditional norms  
- openness to reinterpretation  

A new wave of artists, particularly on Tezos, embraced the toter as both symbol and medium.  

---

## We Are All "Trash" Artists

Trash Art was never about creating a fixed label.  

It was about making a statement:  

> You do not define art anymore.  

It planted itself directly in the tension between “what is” and “what isn’t” art—and refused resolution.  

---

## Four Years Later

And now, four years later, the impact is undeniable.  

The 64-gallon toter is included in TASCHEN’s *On NFTs*.  

A movement once dismissed as unserious is now part of documented art history.  

That shift matters.  

It signals that the boundaries of art didn’t just expand—they were rewritten.
